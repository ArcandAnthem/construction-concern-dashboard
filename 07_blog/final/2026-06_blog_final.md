If your home feels warmer than it should in June, your windows may be working against you.

In Southern California, summer heat is a design problem. Temperatures regularly exceed 90°F in the Santa Clarita Valley, and homes built decades ago were not designed to the energy efficiency standards that make a real difference in today's utility bills.

**The heat gain problem**

Windows can account for 25–30% of residential cooling energy use. In summer, older single-pane windows allow significant solar heat gain into the home, forcing your AC to work harder every day. For homeowners in Santa Clarita, Newhall, and the San Fernando Valley, that can mean $100–200 in additional LADWP costs per month.

**What energy efficient windows do**

Modern energy efficient windows are rated by two key metrics:
- **U-factor:** how much heat passes through the window overall. Lower is better.
- **Solar Heat Gain Coefficient (SHGC):** how much solar radiation enters through the glass. In Southern California's climate, a low SHGC is particularly valuable.

Dual-pane windows with a low-E coating reflect a significant portion of solar radiation before it enters your home.

**Signs your windows are affecting your bill**

- One room consistently runs 8–10°F hotter than others in the afternoon
- Your AC runs nearly continuously but the house never quite cools down
- You notice heat coming through the glass or drafts around the frame in warmer months

**LADWP rebates for window upgrades**

LADWP offers rebates for qualifying window replacements. Most homeowners are not aware these programs exist. Ask us about eligibility when you call — before signing any contract.

Fire season in Southern California doesn't wait for summer to fully arrive.

For homeowners in the Santa Clarita Valley, Newhall, Simi Valley, and the surrounding LA and Ventura County foothills, the risk window typically opens in June and extends through October. Here's a practical checklist of what to look at before risk peaks.

**1. Soffits and eaves**

Open eaves are one of the most common ember entry points in residential fire events. Enclosed soffits, built from fire-resistant materials, significantly reduce this risk. If your home has open eaves or wood soffits, this is the highest-priority exterior upgrade for fire season.

**2. Siding material and condition**

Wood siding has substantially lower ignition resistance than fiber cement, stucco, or Class A-rated alternatives. If your home has older wood siding — particularly on sides facing prevailing wind or adjacent to vegetation — it's worth having a professional assess the material.

**3. Vent screens and openings**

Under-eave vents, attic vents, and crawl space openings are pathways for ember intrusion. Ember-resistant vent screens are inexpensive to install and highly effective. Standard metal mesh screens are typically not ember-resistant.

**4. Sealing exterior gaps**

Small gaps in exterior trim, around window frames, and at the foundation line are pathways for heat and embers. Caulking and sealing these areas is low-cost and worth doing before fire season peaks.

**5. The 30-foot zone**

Make sure there is no wood debris, dry vegetation, or flammable materials stored directly against the exterior walls. This is defensible space — the most basic line of defense.

An HVAC upgrade is one of the most significant home investments a Southern California homeowner can make — and one of the most commonly misunderstood.

**Start with the age of your system**

Most residential HVAC systems have a functional lifespan of 15–20 years. In Southern California, where systems run harder and longer than in most of the country, efficiency tends to degrade earlier. If your system is 12 years or older, have it assessed before it fails in August.

**The envelope problem**

Installing a new HVAC system without addressing the home envelope is a common and expensive mistake. Windows, insulation, and exterior sealing determine how hard your HVAC has to work. A high-efficiency system in a poorly sealed home will underperform its rated efficiency from day one.

The most effective approach: assess the envelope and the HVAC together. Address both in the same project window if the budget allows.

**Rebates most homeowners miss**

LADWP and SoCalGas both offer rebates for qualifying HVAC upgrades. These programs change annually, but high-efficiency systems typically qualify. Ask your contractor about eligibility before committing — many don't mention it unless you ask.

**What to look for in a contractor**

- CSLB verification and a C-20 HVAC license
- Experience with systems common in this climate
- Willingness to discuss the home envelope, not just the unit

**June timing advantage**

Contractors have more availability in June before peak summer demand. Installations completed before July capture efficiency gains for the full summer season.

If you're considering both window replacement and new siding, how you time the project matters more than most homeowners realize.

**The labor overlap**

Window replacement and siding installation share significant labor overlap — scaffolding, exterior access, weatherproofing around the structure. When done separately, that setup and breakdown happens twice. Mobilization costs are duplicated. Bundled together, that overhead is shared, reducing the per-project cost for both items.

**The weatherproofing case**

Windows and siding are not independent systems. They work together to create a sealed exterior. Doing both together allows the contractor to create a properly integrated seal at every window opening — new flashing, trim, and weatherproofing that covers both systems simultaneously. The result is a more durable, better-sealed exterior than either project can achieve alone.

**The visual consistency case**

New windows next to aging siding rarely looks cohesive. New siding framing old window trim is the same problem in reverse. Bundling both gives you a finished exterior that looks intentional and uniform — which matters for curb appeal and resale value.

**Less disruption overall**

Two separate projects mean two periods of exterior work, two sets of cleanup, two periods of contractor access. Homeowners consistently report that bundling is less disruptive overall, even though the scope is larger — because the work happens once and is done.
