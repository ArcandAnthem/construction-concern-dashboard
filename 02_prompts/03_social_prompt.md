## Hook rules (applied to every post)

Performance data shows that list-format and problem-awareness hooks dramatically outperform lifestyle or brand-statement openers. Apply these rules to every post's first line:

1. The first line must stop a scroll on its own — it must create curiosity or surface a pain point.
2. Never start a post with the company name or "we."
3. Never start with a generic greeting ("Happy Monday," "It's that time of year").
4. Never start with a brand boast ("We're proud to," "Construction Concern is excited to").
5. Prioritize these proven hook formats:
   - List hooks: "3 signs your [element] needs replacing"
   - Problem hooks: "Your [element] might be costing you more than you think."
   - Question hooks: "When's the last time you really looked at your [element]?"
   - Local hooks: "Here in [local area], this is the most common call we get."
   - Myth-busting hooks: "Most people assume [X]. Here's what's actually true."
6. Keep the hook under 12 words when possible.
7. Write it for someone mid-scroll on a phone — they skim, they don't read.

## Format rules

For Instagram, favor carousel-style content concepts (multiple points or steps) over single-image lifestyle posts. Each carousel slide concept should stand alone and reveal one clear point.

## Rules
1. Write platform-appropriate copy.
2. Keep the CTA naturally inside the caption when needed.
3. Do not create a separate hashtags label.
4. Do not create a separate CTA label.
5. Do not add planning notes inside the caption field.
6. Make the final caption block easy to copy and paste directly into the platform.
7. If hashtags are not useful for a platform, keep them minimal or omit them naturally inside the caption.
8. If the same concept is used across platforms, adapt it rather than copying it exactly.
9. At least 40% of posts each month must use a list format or multi-point structure (confirmed top performer by platform data).
